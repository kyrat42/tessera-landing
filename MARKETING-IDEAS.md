# Tessera — Marketing Ideas & Survey Quotes

> Last updated: 2026-07-10
> Source: ongoing beta tester survey (see Tessera app repo's beta testing effort)
> Purpose: raw material for landing page copy, positioning, and testimonials — not a task tracker. See the Tessera app repo's `BACKLOG.md` for actionable product bugs/features pulled from this same survey.

---

## Key Takeaways (as of 13 respondents)

**Sample size caveat first**: 13 respondents, with a wide spread in engagement — 4 churned before real usage (Respondents 1, 6, 7, 11), 3 use it regularly but less than daily (4, 5, 12), 2 use it once daily (8, 9), and 4 use it multiple times daily (2, 3, 10, 13). Treat everything below as directional — good for prioritizing what to test next, not a mandate to rewrite the page.

**High confidence — cross-validated by multiple independent respondents:**
1. **"Scattered" is the #1 pain point.** Independently named 4 times by 2+ different respondents ("centralize," "jumbled together," "scattered across different apps," and a genuine before/after: "scattered notes... → a more structured way"). The "centralize everything in one place" pitch is close to confirmed, not speculative.
2. **The areas-of-life/mosaic concept matches how people already think about their lives**, unprompted — one respondent's own life naturally decomposed into work/household/errands/personal, another explicitly compared it to their existing multi-calendar Google setup ("fits really well into your areas of life").
3. **Schedule flexibility is a real, recurring need** — two respondents, two different flavors (self-directed flexible professional vs. externally-imposed shift work). Validates "Routine without rigidity" for more than one persona.
4. **Prioritization difficulty is real friction Tessera resolves** — two respondents named it before using the app; validates the Priority Levels feature specifically.

**Medium confidence — now 3 independent respondents, watch for a 4th before acting:**
5. "External memory / never lose track of your day" — a distinct angle nothing in current copy addresses. Originally single-respondent (R2: "remembering the details of the day"); echoed by Respondent 11 ("just not remembering I had something to do") and now Respondent 13 ("forgetting tasks"). Notable caveats: (1) R11 churned before real usage, so this corroborates the *problem* is real and recurring, not that Tessera's current reminder system resolves it — see the discoverability gap already flagged under FR #15 (reminders are opt-in per-task, easy to miss); (2) 2 of these 3 mentions (R2, R13) are from neurodivergent-identified respondents, so — same caveat as the broader neurodivergence pattern — don't yet treat "external memory" as confirmed to resonate with a general, non-ND audience.
6. "Sense of accomplishment" from progress-tracking (Weekly Hours) — resonates as an emotional payoff, not just a time-tracking utility, in two respondents' own words.
7. The recent onboarding rework appears to be working — one respondent who'd previously churned over "confusing setup" credited a since-shipped update with fixing what blocked them (recurring tasks / multi-day blocks specifically).

**Explicitly unconfirmed — don't act on this yet:**
8. Neurodivergence as a target segment (3 of 5 respondents) is confounded by recruitment channel (sourced partly from an autism community) and survey design (explicitly asks about it). Real theory, not yet evidence.

**Gaps surfaced, not yet in copy:**
9. The "To Schedule" reminder-resurfacing feature solves a named pain point ("forget less urgent responsibilities") but isn't mentioned anywhere on the landing page.

## User Segments & Target Market Trends

Tracks respondent profiles and engagement level, so we can tell target-market signal apart from edge-case feedback. Only trust persona/positioning conclusions drawn from respondents marked **Representative** below.

> **Methodology caveat on the neurodivergence pattern (3 of 5 respondents so far):** this survey pool is not a random/organic sample with respect to neurodivergence. Two compounding selection effects are in play — (1) at least part of the beta tester pool was recruited by promoting the app directly to an autism-specific community, and (2) the survey itself explicitly asks about neurodivergence, which primes respondents to mention it. Both inflate the apparent rate versus what an unprompted, general-audience survey would show. **Treat "neurodivergent users respond well to Tessera" as an open theory to keep testing, not a confirmed target-market finding**, until it shows up from respondents *not* sourced via the autism-group channel, or gets corroborated some other way (e.g. app store reviews, organic word-of-mouth mentions). Don't build landing page copy around this segment on current evidence.

- **Respondent 3**, "what would need to change for you to use Tessera daily" — answer was generic app-review boilerplate ("seamless part of my routine," "compelling reason to return each day," "rewards for consistent engagement") that contradicts their own reported multiple-times-daily usage. - **Respondent 3**, "what made you sign up" — same pattern repeats: "curious about the platform," "something useful and personalized," "fit naturally into my daily routine." - **Status**: two boilerplate-style answers in a row is enough to downgrade this whole respondent's data from representative to uncertain (reflected in the segments table above). Their remaining answers should still be read, but treat any that stay this generic as non-signal; only a genuinely specific, Tessera-particular answer should be pulled back into the main themes table.
- **Respondent 12**, "biggest frustration" — answered "List in notes app and Goodnotes sometimes," which names prior tools rather than describing any frustration. Reads as either a misread question or an answer shifted by one slot. Not counted toward the "scattered" theme tally since no actual pain point is stated; logged for completeness only.
- **Respondent 11**, referral source — answered "email" to how they found the app. This doesn't hold together as a standalone acquisition channel: the landing page's signup form requires entering an email address to begin with, and there's an automated Resend confirmation flow that fires after signup — meaning "email" more likely describes how they received install instructions *after* already signing up somewhere, not how they originally discovered Tessera. Don't log this as a genuine new referral source (alongside Betabound, LinkedIn, the autism community) without a follow-up clarifying where they first heard about it before they visited the site and entered their email.

## Strongest cross-respondent theme so far: "scattered"

Independently mentioned **7 times across 6 respondents**, in different words each time:
- Respondent 1: wanted to "centralize" routines and tasks
- Respondent 2: "everything is jumbled together"
- Respondent 3: "everything felt scattered across different apps" (before Tessera)
- Respondent 3 again: "scattered notes, calendar reminders, and to-do lists" → "a more structured way" (explicitly resolved by Tessera)
- Respondent 6: "outlook and google calendar both suck and do not communicate with each other" — the most concrete version yet, naming an actual mechanism rather than a general feeling
- Respondent 8: "lack of consolidated data" — nearly mirrors Respondent 1's original "centralize" language
- Respondent 11: "phone calendar, outlook calendar and set multiple alarms" — three redundant systems running in parallel, paired with their own admission that they *still* forgot things anyway; the clearest case yet that more tools doesn't fix the underlying problem

This is no longer a single quote to consider — it's the most-validated pain point in the survey by a wide margin, and it crosses engagement tiers, neurotypes, and referral sources. At 7 independent mentions across 6 respondents, this is about as close to confirmed as this kind of data gets. The Respondent 3 mention is a genuine before/after testimonial, which makes the "centralize everything in one place" pitch idea below high-confidence, not speculative — and it's already partially reflected in the current hero subhead. Respondent 11's version also strengthens the case for pairing "centralize" with the "external memory" angle (Key Takeaway #5) rather than treating them as separate ideas — for this respondent specifically, they're the same underlying problem described from two angles.

## Open ideas to explore in copy
- **"Centralize everything in one place" pitch, alongside the existing "balance/mosaic" framing — now backed by 3 independent mentions of the "scattered" pain point (see above). This is the highest-confidence copy idea in this doc so far.**
- Lean into the emotional "before" state (frustration, lack of rest) more explicitly before introducing the mosaic concept.
- Consider surfacing the checklist/completion UX visually as its own selling point, not just implied through screenshots.
- "External memory" / "never lose track of your day" angle — unaddressed by current copy, surfaced by an active daily-use respondent (see segments table). Watch for repetition before elevating to a headline.
- Possible gap: the "To Schedule" reminder-resurfacing feature (incomplete tasks from yesterday reappear on Home) directly solves a named pain point ("forget less urgent responsibilities") but isn't mentioned anywhere in current landing page copy.
- New persona surfaced by Respondent 8: unemployed/job-searching, no external schedule at all. Current "Routine without rigidity" copy assumes an existing schedule that keeps changing — doesn't speak to someone who has no schedule to begin with and needs to build one from scratch. Single respondent so far; watch for repetition before acting.
- **Pricing sensitivity flag (Respondent 12)**: explicitly conditions their ideal planner on not requiring a subscription, in direct tension with Tessera's own planned move to a subscription model. This is a copy/positioning question as much as a pricing one — if subscription pricing lands, messaging may need to work harder to justify the cost (e.g. a generous free tier, or clearly differentiated premium value) for price-sensitive "shopping around" users like this one. Single respondent so far; watch for repetition, but flag early since it's tied to a real, already-decided business direction rather than a hypothetical.
